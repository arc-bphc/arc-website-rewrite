## Welcome to the first issue of our newsletter!

You waited for summer to end, and here it is — right on time.

This summer, ARC went all-in on one idea: **before you build a robot in the real world, you build it a thousand times in simulation.** So we spun up physics engines, wrote controllers, broke them, fixed them, and taught a quadruped to walk from scratch. Here's everything we got up to.

> *"In simulation, gravity is a suggestion, friction is a config file, and every catastrophic failure just costs you a `Ctrl+C`. That's the whole point — fail ten thousand times before breakfast, so the real robot only has to succeed once."*

---

### What We Set Out to Do

We kicked off the break with a plan we called **The Sims** — a five-week climb that takes you from *"what even is a physics engine"* all the way to *"my robot just learned to walk on its own."* No prior robotics required; just curiosity and a willingness to watch your simulated robot faceplant a few hundred times.

The whole curriculum lives here 👉 [**github.com/arc-bphc/the-sims**](https://github.com/arc-bphc/the-sims)

*   **Weeks 1–2 — Spawn, Control, Move:** We dropped robots into **MuJoCo**, got comfortable with the **`.mjcf`** format (a cousin of URDF/xacro, not the same thing), inspected mass/joint/actuator limits and sensor configs, sent raw torque commands, logged proprioceptive sensor data, and wired up sliders to drive joints by hand. Then came the math that makes limbs move *on purpose* — forward and inverse kinematics derived geometrically, cubic-spline end-effector trajectories for the **SO-100** arm, and a **sine-wave gait** for the **Pupper**: vary each foot's X and Z sinusoidally, offset the phase per leg, and the dog walks.
*   **Weeks 3–4 — The RL Foundations:** We worked through the **HuggingFace Deep RL Course** — value functions, policy gradients, actor-critic, and **PPO** — so that nothing in the training code later would feel like a black box.
*   **Week 5 — Make It Walk:** The payoff. Real reinforcement learning for quadruped locomotion on the **mjlab + RSL_RL** stack, with training runs tracked live on **Weights & Biases**.

### Weeks 3–4: Learning How Robots Learn

Before you can train a robot, you have to understand what "learning" even means to one. Weeks three and four were spent inside the HuggingFace Deep RL Course, building up the vocabulary — states, actions, rewards, and the value functions that tie them together.

Here's the classic example that makes it click: a mouse, a maze, and some cheese. The **action-value function** asks *"from this square, if I take this step, how good is my future?"* — and every cell gets a number that answers it.

### Week 5: Teaching a Go1 to Walk — with Reinforcement Learning

This is where it all comes together. Getting a quadruped to walk with RL means five libraries each doing exactly one job:

*   **MuJoCo** simulates the physics — bodies, contacts, gravity, torques.
*   **MuJoCo Warp** runs *thousands* of those simulations in parallel on the GPU.
*   **mjlab** wraps that raw physics into an RL problem — the observations, actions, rewards, and terminations. **This is where almost all the engineering effort goes.**
*   **PyTorch** is the tensor language everything speaks.
*   **RSL_RL** (from ETH Zürich) runs the actual PPO learning loop — the same algorithm from Weeks 3–4, just batched across thousands of robots at once.

But before you train anything, you make sure the plumbing actually works. mjlab ships with demos you can run straight out of the box — so our very first move was to load a **pretrained model and just hit play**, watching a humanoid dance across the viewer. No training, no reward tuning — purely a smoke test to confirm the GPU, the install, and the whole stack were talking to each other:

Once the demo danced, we knew the stack was live. Time to actually train something.

Our first milestone was the **Unitree Go1**, which ships with a ready-made mjlab environment. Training it end-to-end was how we proved the *learning* side of the stack works before building anything of our own:

```bash
# Train the shipped Go1 velocity task across 512 parallel robots
uv run train Mjlab-Velocity-Flat-Unitree-Go1 --env.scene.num-envs 512
```

Then you sit back and watch the reward curves climb on Weights & Biases — the moment a flailing pile of joints turns into a walk:

</video>

The real work isn't running that command, though — it's **building the environment itself**. That means deciding what the robot senses (its 12 joint angles and velocities, how its torso is tilted, its foot position vectors, its last action) and what earns it reward. And here's the twist that trips everyone up: the policy never outputs torque. It outputs a small offset to a default joint position, which a built-in PD controller turns into torque:

```
policy output  →  scaled offset  →  target joint position  →  PD controller  →  torque  →  physics
```

Every reward is a weighted sum of hand-designed terms — stay upright, hold a target torso height, track the commanded velocity, don't waste energy, don't drag your feet. Get the weights wrong and the robot "cheats": drop the height penalty and it happily learns to crawl. That tuning — the observations, the foot vectors, the reward terms — is the summer's real engineering, and it's exactly what carries us from the Go1 into building our own quadruped environments from scratch.

RL doesn't hand you a working robot on day one. It hands you a robot that's slightly less terrible than yesterday's — and if you stack enough of those days, you get something that walks.

### Sneak Peek: The CNI Micromouse Task

We weren't just building for ourselves — a big chunk of the summer went into designing the tasks that'll welcome this year's **incoming juniors**. And the one we're most excited about brings that mouse-and-cheese gridworld to life: **a micromouse maze solver.**

Except this mouse is a real robot in simulation, and it's driven entirely through **ROS topics**. It doesn't get a map. It has to *subscribe* to its wall sensors to feel out its surroundings, reason about which way is open, and *publish* velocity commands to actually move — feeling its way to the center of the maze one cell at a time.

```
   sensor topics  ──▶  [ your maze-solving node ]  ──▶  velocity command topic
   (what walls?)          (flood fill? wall follow?)        (drive!)
```

Flood fill? Wall follower? Something smarter? That's for the juniors to figure out. Here's a first look at the task in action:
